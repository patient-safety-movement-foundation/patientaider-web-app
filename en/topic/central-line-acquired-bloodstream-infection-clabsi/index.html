Central Line-Acquired Bloodstream Infection (CLABSI)

An infection that happens from a central catheter. A central catheter is a tube in a large vein in the neck, chest or groin. 1

Embedded content: https://youtu.be/sFROH5rvWOY

Symptoms:

  • Redness near the tube
  • Red streaks near the tube
  • Swelling
  • Warmth
  • Yellow or green liquid
  • Pain or soreness
  • Fever
  • Chills 1

Guide to Medical Care at a Distance

Don’t touch where your IV lines are.

When a central catheter IV line is inserted, make sure there is:

  • Careful hand washing
  • Barrier precautions used during line insertion (including hair cover, mask, sterile gloves, sterile gown, eye protection and drape)
  • Cleaning the skin with chlorhexidine gluconate skin prep
  • The skin prep needs to be dry before inserting the central line
  • Avoid using the femoral vein (upper leg near the groin)
  • Daily review of the need for the central catheter IV and prompt removal of unnecessary catheters.34

Embedded content: https://youtu.be/-DNuFp6KDVM

All healthcare providers and visitors must wash their hands. The average handwashing rate is 40% for healthcare providers in the hospital.5 Never be afraid to ask your doctor or nurse to please wash their hands!

1. Bring in hand sanitizer for the patient, healthcare providers, and visitors to use. Ask your doctor or nurse to wash their hands with you.6

2. Use disinfectant wipes on all surfaces that you or the patient will come in contact with. Ask the nurse for these.7

3. Put up a sign asking staff to wash their hands on your door and above your bed.6

Embedded content: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=M8AKTACyiB0

4. Make sure doctors and nurses wipe the round end of the stethoscope with an alcohol wipe.7

5. Gloves need to be worn when there is contact with blood or other body fluids, mouth, nose, lungs, urinary and digestive tracts, open skin areas, or items that are soiled with blood or other body fluids. Healthcare workers need to wash their hands both before and after using gloves. Wearing gloves is never a substitute for hand washing!6 Gloves can have microscopic tears and holes.

6. Healthcare providers and visitors need to follow Isolation Precautions of wearing gowns, gloves and sometimes masks if the patient's door has an Isolation Precaution sign on it.

Isolation Precaution Information from the National Library of Medicine

7. The patient must wash their hands before eating every meal and before going to sleep.6

8. The patient needs toothbrushing, &/or denture cleaning, & mouth rinsing 2-4 times every day.8

9. Deep breathing, incentive spirometer & coughing exercises every hour while awake. The patient should do at least 10 deep breaths every hour using the incentive spirometer while in bed.9

10. When is the soonest the patient can safely get out of bed & walk?8

11. Cellphones are dirty1011

  • Wipe your cell phone with an alcohol wipe.
  • Do not take a cell phone into the bathroom.
  • Do not place the cell phone on the floor, especially the bathroom floor.

12. Cover your mouth when you cough or sneeze. Use your elbow or arm instead of your hands.

13. When a nurse is giving IV medications, make sure the nurse cleans the IV port for 15 seconds before and after with an alcohol wipe.12 Don’t touch where your IV lines meet your skin.1

14. Tell your nurse and doctor if there is pain, swelling, redness, itching or wetness at an IV line or incision.6

Embedded content: https://youtu.be/67vDXQMxUz4

15. Ask if you or the patient can have their own blood pressure cuff to be used only by them.6

16. Remove your slippers worn over socks before getting back into your bed. This prevents germs from the floor coming into your bed.6

17. All visitors need to6:

  • Wash their hands before entering and leaving the room
  • Do not visit if they feel ill
  • Avoid using the patient’s bathroom
