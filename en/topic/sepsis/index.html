Sepsis

About 270,000 people die from sepsis each year in the United States.1 That is equal to one person dying every 2 minutes. About 87% of sepsis cases begin outside of the hospital.1 Sepsis kills more people than breast cancer, prostate cancer and AIDS combined2

Sepsis is the body's extreme and life-threatening overreaction to an infection. The body attacks itself and can cause tissue damage, organ failure, amputations and death.2

Questions to Ask:

  • Does the patient have a fast heart rate?
  • Is the patient breathing fast?
  • Does the patient have a very low blood pressure?
  • Does the patient have a fever or low body temperature?
  • Is the patient's skin pale or discolored?
  • Does the patient have extreme pain?
  • Is the patient sleepy, confused or difficult to wake?
  • Is there an increase or decrease in white blood cells?2

Post-Sepsis Syndrome (PSS)

Embedded content: https://youtu.be/HIk64wdy44Q

Fifty percent (50%) of Sepsis survivors suffer from Post-Sepsis Syndrome.3

New Physical Challenges:

  • Fatigue and weakness
  • Muscle &/or joint pain
  • Amputation
  • Increased risk for infection
  • Shortness of breath
  • Dressing, grooming, walking, & feeding difficulties3,4

New Mental Issues:

  • Depression, anxiety, &/or PTSD (Post-traumatic stress syndrome)
  • Poor memory or slow thinking
  • Difficulty concentrating
  • Difficulty sleeping, nightmares, &/or insomnia
  • Panic attacks
  • Hallucinations3,4

Prevention:

  • Early mobilization
  • ICU Diary
  • Family/Caregiver support and engagement5,6

Treatments & Support:

  • Physical, Occupational, &/or Speech therapy
  • Psychological therapy, counseling, &/or neuropsychological testing
  • Follow up health care provider &/or specialist appointments
  • Medication review & education by health care provider5,6
