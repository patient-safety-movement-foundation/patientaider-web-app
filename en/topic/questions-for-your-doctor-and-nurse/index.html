Ask Questions!

No question is too trivial to ask. Trust your instincts.

Guide to Medical Care at a Distance

2. Bring a family member or advocate to help listen and speak up for you. Embedded content: https://youtu.be/MEFJfpKslQg

3. Bring the patient's medication and allergy list to show the doctor and nurse.2

Personal Medicine List from ISMP

4. Don't distract the nurse while they are giving medications. Ask questions before!1

5 Questions to Ask About Your Medications from ISMP Canada

Taking Medications- What to Ask Your Doctor from MedlinePlus

5. Ask questions respectfully about everything: 2

  • Ask for a drug information sheet with every new medication.
  • Why do I need this medication?
  • What is the medication for?
  • What will it do?
  • When do I take the first dose?
  • How often should this medication be taken?
  • How much should be taken?
  • What does "as needed" mean?
  • How do you get refills?
  • When will I stop taking this medication?
  • What are the side effects &/or possible drug interactions?
  • Where do I get the medication?
  • Will my insurance pay for this medication?
  • Are there any foods or drinks that should be avoided?
  • Can I drive after taking this medication?
  • Tell your doctor if you stop taking this or any medication.
  • Can you explain that to me again?

6. Write in your phone, mobile device, computer or notebook with the answers given:1

  • What is the test, procedure, medication and/or surgery for?
  • How many times have you done this test or procedure?
  • When will the results be back?
  • Why do I need this treatment?
  • Are there any alternatives?
  • What are the complications?

6 Tips for Talking With Your Doctor from CAPS

11. At the change of shift, ask the patient's nurses to compare notes at the patient's bedside.2

12. Call the nurse if the patient doesn't seem right or if the alarm goes off. 12

13. Call the nurse if the patient has: 12

  • Extreme pain
  • Fever or chills
  • Redness, swelling, &/or pus
  • Confusion
  • Difficulty breathing
  • Very slow breathing
  • Dizziness
  • Unusual sweating
  • Rash or hives
  • Drop in blood pressure
  • High or low temperature
  • Changes in heart rate
  • Changes in breathing
  • Decrease in urine
  • Patient doesn’t look right

Listen to your gut or intuition. If no one is listening to you, speak with the Nurse Manager. 12

Adult and children normal vital signs are below: 56

  • Normal body temperature is 97.8 degrees Fahrenheit to 99 degrees Fahrenheit.
  • Normal adult heart rate is 60 to 100 beats per minute.
  • Normal adult breathing rate is 12 to 16 breathes per minute.
  • Normal adult blood pressure is less than 120/80 mm Hg.

Medical Device Questions:7

  • How many procedures have you done with this device?
  • Have any patients had problems?
  • Have you ever removed this device?
  • Has this device ever been recalled?
  • What are the risks and alternative treatments?
  • Does this device have a black box warning or contraindications?
  • What is the device made of, and what can be done if an adverse reaction to the materials occurs?
  • Have you had any financial relationship with the manufacturer of this device?
  1. Research any device that will be used on or in you. 7
  2. Get a 2nd opinion for any risky or expensive procedure.7
  3. Find out if the device has had any recalls.7
  4. Look at ProPublica’s Dollars for Devices or Open Payments Data from CMS to find out if your MD has received money from the device company.7
  5. Research your MD’s experience with medical devices at ProPublica’s Surgeon Scorecard.7

Healthcare Providers

  • Write down or have someone write down the medical staff’s names if no card is given.1
  • Be aware that the medical team may not speak with each other, as they should.1
