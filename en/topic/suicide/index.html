Suicide

Suicide is a major public health crisis in the US.

It is the 2nd leading cause of death for 15-34 year olds and the 10th leading cause of death in the United States.1

Embedded content: https://youtu.be/tUxvgL2rqMw

The suicide rate in the US continues to increase every year. Every 12 minutes, someone dies from suicide in the United States.1

Once a day, 20 veterans die from suicide in the United States.15

Learn to See the Signs:16234567

  • Sad or depressed most of the time
  • Deep sadness, loss of interest, trouble sleeping & eating all the time or getting worse
  • Anxious, agitated or not sleeping
  • Pulling away from family and friends or sleeping all the time
  • No interest in hobbies, work, school or things they care about
  • Frequent & dramatic mood changes
  • Feeling very guilty or ashamed
  • Feeling life isn’t worth living, having no purpose to live
  • Feeling trapped-no way out of their situation
  • Feeling desperate & no answers to their problems
  • Not acting like themselves

Warning Signs:16234567891011

  • Doing poorly at work or school
  • Violent actions: punching holes in walls, getting into fights, feeling rage, uncontrolled anger, &/or seeking revenge
  • Acting like they have a “death wish”, tempting fate by driving fast or running red lights
  • Not responding to verbal intervention
  • Looking for ways to kill themselves

Dangerous Signs:234567891011

  • Immediate danger to themselves or others
  • Talking about ways to kill themselves
  • Making a plan to kill themselves
  • Giving away prized possessions
  • Putting affairs in order, tying up loose ends, &/or making out a will
  • Getting firearms, drugs, or other ways to kill themselves

Triggers:234567891011

  • End of a relationship or marriage
  • Death of a loved one
  • An arrest
  • Serious financial problems

If you know someone with any of these signs, get help by calling:

The National Suicide Prevention Lifeline: 1-800-273-TALK (8255), confidential help 24-hours-a-day or call 911.

Veterans Crisis Line : 1–800–273–8255, press 1

Crisis Text Line : text START to 741–741

SAMSHA National Helpline is confidential, free, 24 hour, 365 days treatment referral service (English & Spanish): Treatment Referral Routing Service: 1–800–662–HELP (4357) from SAMSHA (Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration)

SAMSHA Behavioral Health Treatment Services Locator

Follow your instincts. If you can’t de-escalate your loved one, then it is most likely a mental health emergency.

If you are worried, ASK FOR HELP!

Don’t wait and don’t leave your loved one alone. Act quickly in a crisis.

Risk Factors:16234567891011

  • Prior suicide attempt(s) &/or self-injury behavior
  • Depression, anxiety, schizophrenia &/or alcohol/substance abuse
  • PTSD, ADHD, TBI (traumatic brain injury) &/or antisocial behavior
  • Chronic pain
  • Having hallucinations
  • Increased alcohol and/or drug use
  • Family history of suicide
  • Family violence
  • Easy access to firearms
  • Recent release from jail or prison
  • Serious or chronic medical illness
  • Being around suicidal behavior
  • Men are 4 times more likely to die from suicide
  • History of trauma &/or abuse
  • Perfectionist personality
  • Gay, Lesbian & Trans youth
  • Learning disabled youth
  • Feeling disgraced or ashamed to ask for help
  • Lack of healthcare especially mental health & substance abuse treatment
  • Recent discharge from psychiatric hospital, provider or treatment change
  • Low self-esteem
  • Prolonged stress
  • Isolation
  • Recent tragedy

Challenges After Hospital Discharge:23456789101112

  • Not attending follow up appointments
  • No access or unaware of mental health resources
  • No family &/or friends for support
  • Inappropriate or unidentified care referrals

Prevention:234567891011121314

  • Speak up if you’re worried
  • Be yourself
  • Listen
  • Be sympathetic
  • Offer hope
  • Take your loved one or friend seriously
  • Don’t argue
  • Don’t act shocked
  • Don’t promise to keep this to yourself
  • Don’t offer ways to fix their problems
  • Don’t give advice
  • Make a Crisis Plan
  • Make a Comfort Kit & Plan Use the Hospital Plan of Care form to advocate and ask questions about your loved one.

Guide to Medical Care at a Distance

Help your loved one/friend make a Crisis Plan:13

  1. Phone numbers for your loved one’s therapist, psychiatrist and other healthcare providers
  2. Local crisis line number (contact your NAMI Affiliate, or do an internet search for “mental health crisis services” and the name of your county)
  3. Phone numbers of family members or friends who would be helpful in a crisis
  4. Addresses of walk-in crisis centers or emergency rooms
  5. The National Suicide Prevention Lifeline: 1-800-273-TALK (8255)
  6. Your address and phone number(s)
  7. Your loved one’s diagnosis and medications
  8. Previous psychosis or suicide attempts
  9. History of drug use
  10. Triggers
  11. Things that have helped in the past
  12. Mobile Crisis Unit phone number in the area (if there is one)
  13. Determine if police officers in the community have Crisis Intervention Training (CIT)
  14. Go over the plan with your loved one
  15. Keep copies in several places
  16. Store a copy in a drawer in your kitchen, your car, on your phone, or in your wallet
  17. Keep a copy in a room in your home that has a lock and a phone

Comfort Plan: 14

  1. This plan is a way to cope with intense emotions
  2. Helps you to feel more comfortable and safe
  3. Identifies your main challenges
  4. What your strong emotions look and feel like
  5. Healthy ways (grounding tools) to deal with your challenges and intense emotions
  6. Learn what level your emotions are at
  7. How others can help
  8. Crisis happens when emotions go outside the "window of tolerance" and feel unmanageable

Comfort Plan Guide from Vancouver Coastal Health

How to Make Your Plan: 14

  1. Write down as many ideas as you can
  2. Ask others for ideas
  3. Think about how you feel before and during a crisis
  4. How you look to others during a crisis
  5. What can you and others do when you are in crisis
  6. What can you do to prevent having a crisis

What is a Comfort Kit? 14

  1. Collection of grounding tools that you put in a shoebox size container.
  2. Gather healthy items, sayings and reminders to help ground yourself
  3. Your "emergency kit" or "fire extinguisher" for strong negative emotions

How to Use a Comfort Plan & Kit? 14

  1. Keep your plan & kit in an easy to find place
  2. Share your kit & plan with others
  3. Update your plan & kit as you build your skills

Comfort Kit

Ways to Help:234567891011121314

  • Get professional help & help your loved one/friend connect
  • Follow-up on treatment
  • Be proactive-drop by, call, ask them out
  • Remove/reduce ways of self-harm-pills, guns, knives and razors
  • Encourage healthy lifestyle changes
  • Be there for them
  • Help them make a Crisis Plan
  • Help them make a Comfort Plan & Kit
