Catheter-Associated Urinary Tract Infection (CAUTI)

An infection that happens when a urinary catheter is in your bladder. 2

Embedded content: https://youtu.be/CXJleBIGQQ8

Symptoms:

  • Cloudy or dark urine color
  • Blood in the urine
  • Foul urine smell
  • Need to urinate frequently
  • Pain, pressure or spasms in back or low belly
  • Fever or chills
  • Confusion
  • Flank pain45

Call the nurse if the patient “doesn’t seem right” or if the alarm goes off.45

Listen to your gut or intuition. If no one is listening to you, speak with the Nurse Manager.

Guide to Medical Care at a Distance

1. When will the urinary catheter be removed?4

2. Urinary catheters should be removed within 24 hours of insertion. If a urinary catheter needs to be inserted, it must be done with aseptic technique, sterile equipment and properly secured.3

3. All healthcare providers and visitors must wash their hands. The average handwashing rate is 40% for healthcare providers in the hospital.6 Never be afraid to ask your doctor or nurse to please wash their hands!

Embedded content: https://youtu.be/tZnKlFtAUDw

Embedded content: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l4HpcOpQaoQ

4. Bring in hand sanitizer for the patient, healthcare providers, and visitors to use. Ask your doctor or nurse to wash their hands with you. 4

5. Use disinfectant wipes on all surfaces that you or the patient will come in contact with. Ask the nurse for these.7

6. Put up a sign telling staff to wash their hands on your door and above your bed.4

7. Make sure doctors and nurses wipe the round end of the stethoscope with an alcohol wipe.7

Embedded content: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=M8AKTACyiB0

8. Gloves need to be worn when there is contact with blood or other body fluids, mouth, nose, lungs, urinary and digestive tracts, open skin areas, or items that are soiled with blood or other body fluids. Healthcare workers need to wash their hands both before and after using gloves. Wearing gloves is never a substitute for hand washing!4 Gloves can have microscopic tears and holes.

9. Healthcare providers and visitors need to follow Isolation Precautions of wearing gowns, gloves and sometimes masks if the patient's door has an Isolation Precaution sign on it.

Isolation Precaution Information from the National Library of Medicine

10. The patient must wash their hands before eating every meal and before going to sleep.4

11. The patient needs toothbrushing, &/or denture cleaning, & mouth rinsing 2-4 times every day.1

12. Deep breathing, incentive spirometer & coughing exercises every hour while awake. The patient should do at least 10 deep breaths every hour using the incentive spirometer while in bed.8

13. When is the soonest the patient can safely get out of bed & walk?1

14. Cellphones are dirty. 910

  • Wipe your cell phone with an alcohol wipe.
  • Do not take a cell phone into the bathroom.
  • Do not place the cell phone on the floor, especially the bathroom floor.

15. Cover your mouth when you cough or sneeze. Use your elbow or arm instead of your hands.

16. All visitors need to4:

  • Wash their hands before entering and leaving the room
  • Do not visit if they feel ill
  • Avoid using the patient’s bathroom

17. When a nurse is giving IV medications, make sure the nurse cleans the IV port for 15 seconds before and after with an alcohol wipe.11 Don’t touch where your IV lines meet your skin.12

18. Tell your nurse and doctor if there is pain, swelling, redness, itching or wetness at an IV line or incision.4

Embedded content: https://youtu.be/67vDXQMxUz4

19. Ask if you or the patient can have their own blood pressure cuff to be used only by them.4

20. Remove your slippers worn over socks before getting back into your bed. This prevents germs from the floor coming into your bed.4
