New Medications 4:

  • Ask for a drug information sheet with every new medication.
  • Why do I need this medication?
  • What is the medication for?
  • What will it do?
  • When do I take the first dose?
  • How often should this medication be taken?
  • How much should be taken?
  • What does "as needed" mean?
  • How do you get refills?
  • When will I stop taking this medication?
  • What are the side effects &/or possible drug interactions?
  • Where do I get the medication?
  • Will my insurance pay for this medication?
  • Are there any foods or drinks that should be avoided?
  • Can I drive after taking this medication?
  • Tell your doctor if you stop taking this or any medication.
  • Can you explain that to me again?

Call your Doctor if you have any of these symptoms from a medication 3:

  • Falling asleep mid-sentence
  • Confusion or dizziness
  • Itching, rash or hives
  • Severe pain
  • Headache
  • Stomach ache
  • Loss of muscle control
  • Increased or decreased heart rate
  • Nausea, constipation or diarrhea
  • Low or high blood pressure

Call 911 with these symptoms:

  • Difficulty breathing
  • Breathing too slowly
  • Difficulty speaking
  • Seizure
  • Partial or total paralysis
  • Swelling of lips, tongue or face
  • Vision loss
  • Loss of muscle control

Storing Your Medicines 45:

  • Store your medications out of reach of children and pets. 
  • Always put away every medication every time it’s used.
  • Make sure the safety cap is locked.
  • Use a child-proof container when children are in the home.
  • Teach children about medication safety.
  • Teach your guests about medication safety.
  • Program the Poison Help number 1-800-222-1222 into your phone. 
  • Never tell a child that a medication is candy.

How to Dispose of Medications 6:

It’s Important to Safely Remove Opioids From Your Home by FDA

  • If a drug disposal place isn’t available, look at the medication label to see if it is on the FDA Flush List.
  • If the medication isn’t on the FDA Flush List, mix inside a plastic bag with  dirt, used coffee grounds, or cat litter. Seal the bag and throw it out  in your trash. Delete all personal information on the bottle or packaging and recyle or trash the empty bottle or package.
  • Inhaler labels should have disposal instructions. Call your local pharmacy or recycling facility for help. Inhalers can explode or leak medication if punctured or thrown in a fire.
