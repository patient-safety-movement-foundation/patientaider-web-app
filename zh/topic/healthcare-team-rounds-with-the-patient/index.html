Healthcare Team Rounds with the Patient

What are Healthcare Team Rounds?

These rounds are daily conversations between the patient, family members, and key hospital staff of physicians, nurses, therapists, social workers, and pharmacists. These rounds serve as a coordination plan to ensure the best patient care and involvement.

What do daily Healthcare Team rounds look like?

  • Rounds should be at the same time each day, so that families can be there
  • Everyone should be prepared, on time, and present

What should be discussed?

While each team member will have different talking points, the following should almost always be addressed by someone:

  • Plan of care
  • Goals of care
  • Expected length of stay
  • Expected outcomes for the day
  • Discharge plan
  • Delays or challenges
  • Patient progress
  • Issues related to the family
  • Changes in care
  • Any insurance barriers related to discharge
  • Pending insurance approvals
  • Behavioral health service assessment
  • Community referral status

Why are Healthcare Team rounds beneficial?

These rounds can ensure that the patient and family members are on the same page as their hospital team to:

  • Improve teamwork
  • Improve communication
  • Reduce replication & redundancy
  • Improve patient experience
  • Reduce errors
  • Optimize discharge & post-hospitalization care
  • Increase satisfaction among all team members

How can patients and family members participate?

  • Ask questions
  • Voice concerns & comments
  • Write down goals, concerns, & questions before rounds

Guide to Medical Care at a Distance

Why is patient and family participation important?

This family involvement should be present in every moment while in the hospital. Family members know what “normal” looks like for their loved one. The Healthcare Team does not know the patient like the family does.

While the team may be exceptional, your voice adds an important, unique element to the Healthcare Team rounds. Patient and family participation can prevent errors, improve patient comfort, and help in understanding of the patient.

Healthcare Team

Doctors

  • Hospitalist: A medical doctor who only cares for patients in a hospital.
  • Attending Physician: A medical doctor in charge of and responsible for your care.
  • Fellow: A medical doctor training to be a specialist and has completed a residency program.
  • Resident: A medical doctor who practices medicine under the attending doctor and has completed the first year (intern) of a residency program.
  • Intern: A medical doctor who is in the first year of a residency program.

Nurses, Nurse Practitioners and Assistants

  • Nurse Manager: Manages the floor or unit’s daily operations.
  • Nurse Practitioner/Physician Assistant: Practices medicine under the supervision of a doctor. Works closely with your attending doctor to coordinate your care
  • Registered Nurse: Provide 24-hour bedside care and assistance.
  • Patient Care Assistant/Technician: Performs many important tasks and patient care activities under the direction of a registered nurse.

Other Healthcare Team Members

  • Case/Care Manager: A nurse or social worker who follows and supports the patient and family during the hospital stay. Coordinates discharge planning.
  • Respiratory Therapist: Evaluates, monitors and treats patients with breathing problems. Assists with mechanical ventilation.
  • Physical Therapist: Evaluates and helps patients improve their strength and mobility with exercises.
  • Occupational Therapist: Evaluates and helps patients with coordination and motor skills for daily living skills.
  • Speech Therapist: Evaluates and monitors a patient’s ability to swallow and eat.
  • Dietitian: Evaluates a patient’s nutrition needs.
  • Patient Support Associate: Assists with the daily operation of the critical care unit.
  • Chaplain: Provides emotional and spiritual support to patients and their families.
  • Patient Advocacy/Patient Relations: Hospital staff member who helps a patient and family when problems occur.
  • Patient Sitter/Observer: Sits with and observes patients who are suicidal, homicidal, confused, combative or at risk for falling.2345

Rapid Response Team

A team of healthcare providers who go to the bedside of a rapidly declining patient to assess and treat to prevent an intensive care transfer, cardiac arrest or death.

Any staff member (at some hospitals a patient or family member) can call the team with these signs:6

  • Heart rate greater than 140 beats per minute or less than 40 beats per minute
  • Breathing or respiratory rate greater than 28 breaths per minute or less than 8 per minute
  • Systolic blood pressure greater than 180 mmHg or less than 90 mmHg
  • Oxygen saturation less than 90% with supplemental oxygen
  • Acute mental status change
  • Urine output less than 50cc over 4 hours
  • Chest pain unrelieved by nitroglycerin
  • Threatened airway
  • Seizure
  • Uncontrolled pain
