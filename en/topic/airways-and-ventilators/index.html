Airways and Ventilators

When a patient can’t breathe, or needs help breathing an artificial airway is put in. This process is called intubation. The airway is attached to a ventilator (breathing machine).5

There are 2 kinds of artificial airways:

  • An endotracheal tube is placed in the windpipe (trachea) by going through the mouth or nose.
  • A tracheostomy tube is connected to a surgical hole in the neck.5

Monitors measure the heart rate, breathing rate, blood pressure and oxygen level. It is recommended that the exhaled carbon dioxide level (ETCO2) is measured by capnography. Normal ETCO2 range is 35-40 mmHg.6

Communicating with the patient is much more difficult, because the patient can’t speak unless they have a tracheostomy tube with a speaking valve. Having an airway can cause anxiety, fear and discomfort for a patient.7

Patients can communicate by:7

  • Writing on paper
  • Nodding or shaking head
  • Using hand or facial gestures
  • Pointing to letters, pictures or phrases on paper or a board
  • Texting
  • Patient Communicator mobile app from SCCM

Alert the nurse if:

  • The airway comes out
  • The patient’s monitor alarms go off
  • Increased breathing difficulty
  • Changes in breathing
  • The patient looks worse
  • The airway doesn’t look or sound right
  • Increased confusion

Complications for patients on ventilators:89

  • Ventilator-associated pneumonia
  • Sepsis
  • Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS)
  • Pulmonary Embolism
  • Lung damage
  • Pulmonary edema
  • Fluid overload

Guide to Medical Care at a Distance

  • Help to make sure the patient's back and head are always more than a 30 degree angle.
  • When can the patient breathe on their own?
  • When can the sedation medication be reduced?
  • When can the patient get out of bed & move around?
  • Tell the nurse if the patient slides down in the bed.
  • Does the patient have pressure sores on their back and/or buttocks?
  • Can the patient have a subglottic secretion drainage endotracheal tube if the ventilator will be in more than 72 hours?
  • Are the patient's teeth &/or dentures being cleaned twice a day?
  • Is the patient's mouth being rinsed with a non-alcohol based mouth rinse?
  • If the patient is at risk for aspiration will their teeth & mouth be cleaned 2-4 times a day with a suction toothbrush kit?
  • Is the patient being suctioned every 4 hours?

1. Bring in hand sanitizer for the patient, healthcare providers, and visitors to use. Ask your doctor or nurse to wash their hands with you.

Embedded content: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=M8AKTACyiB0

2. Use disinfectant wipes on all surfaces that you or the patient will come in contact with. Ask the nurse for these.10

3. Put up a sign telling staff to wash their hands on your door and above your bed.10

4. Make sure doctors and nurses wipe the round end of the stethoscope with an alcohol wipe.10

5. Gloves need to be worn when there is contact with blood or other body fluids, mouth, nose, lungs, urinary and digestive tracts, open skin areas, or items that are soiled with blood or other body fluids. Healthcare workers need to wash their hands both before and after using gloves. Wearing gloves is never a substitute for hand washing!11 Gloves can have microscopic tears and holes.

6. Healthcare providers and visitors need to follow Isolation Precautions of wearing gowns, gloves and sometimes masks if the patient's door has an Isolation Precaution sign on it.

Isolation Precaution Information from the National Library of Medicine

7. The patient must wash their hands before eating every meal and before going to sleep.11

8. Cellphones are dirty1213

  • Wipe your cell phone with an alcohol wipe.
  • Do not take a cell phone into the bathroom.
  • Do not place the cell phone on the floor, especially the bathroom floor.

9. Cover your mouth when you cough or sneeze. Use your elbow or arm instead of your hands.

10. When a nurse is giving IV medications, make sure the nurse cleans the IV port for 15 seconds before and after with an alcohol wipe.14 Don’t touch where your IV lines meet your skin.15

11. Tell your nurse and doctor if there is pain, swelling, redness, itching or wetness at an IV line or incision.

Embedded content: https://youtu.be/67vDXQMxUz4

12. Ask if you or the patient can have their own blood pressure cuff to be used only by them.11

13. Remove your slippers worn over socks before getting back into your bed. This prevents germs from the floor coming into your bed.11

14. All visitors need to:11

  • Wash their hands before entering and leaving the room
  • Do not visit if they feel ill
  • Avoid using the patient’s bathroom
