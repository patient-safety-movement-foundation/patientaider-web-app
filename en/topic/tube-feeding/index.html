Tube Feeding

Tube feeding (enteral nutrition) is given when a patient21:

  • Canâ€™t swallow or chew
  • Not absorbing nutrients in digestive system
  • Illness
  • Decreased appetite

Infant and pediatric patients get tube feedings for3:

  • Absent or weak sucking or swallowing reflex
  • Birth defects
  • Electrolyte imbalances
  • Elimination problems

Feeding Tubes

Feeding tubes are inserted to give the tube feeding (enteral nutrition).

Types of feeding tubes45:

  • Nasogastric (NG) put through the nose into the stomach
  • Percutaneous endoscopic gastrostomy (PEG) put through the skin into the stomach
  • Jejunostomy (J-tube) put through the skin into the small bowel

Risk factors before tube insertion67:

  • Altered mental status
  • Sedative or pain medications
  • Tracheostomy or endotracheostomy tube
  • Absent cough reflex
  • Anatomic abnormalities
  • ICU patient

Risks when inserting feeding tubes678:

  • Wrong tube placement into the lungs, esophagus or brain
  • Reaction to anesthesia
  • Pain
  • Leakage of stomach contents
  • Infection at tube insertion
  • Bleeding at tube insertion
  • Bleeding and/or infection in other organs in the body
  • Blockage of the tube
  • Tube moves out of the stomach or small bowel

Ways to verify the tube is in the right place6789:

  • X-ray for blind insertion of tube and for ETP (electromagnetic tube placement)
  • Measure pH of aspirate from tube
  • Capnography
  • Watch for signs of respiratory distress
  • Auscultation method (air bolus) is not accurate
  • Water bubbling method is not accurate
  • Measure aspirate volume moving from small bowel to stomach
  • Review chest and abdomen x-rays for tube moving

Problems with feeding tubes6789:

  • Tube feeding goes into the lungs (aspiration)
  • Clogging
  • Wrong connections to IVs or other catheters
  • Moves to esophagus or lung
  • Infections
  • Bleeding
