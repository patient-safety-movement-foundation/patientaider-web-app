Medication Errors

A medication error is when a patient gets the wrong medication, the wrong medication amount, the medication in the wrong way, &/or gets the medication at the wrong time.

About 1.5 million preventable medication errors occur each year in US hospitals.4

About 4,110 patients a day!

Any of these symptoms can be from a medication error5:

  • Falling asleep mid-sentence
  • Confusion or dizziness
  • Difficulty breathing
  • Breathing too slowly
  • Itching, rash or hives
  • Partial or total paralysis
  • Severe pain
  • Headache
  • Stomach ache
  • Seizure
  • Loss of muscle control
  • Difficulty speaking
  • Increased or decreased heart rate
  • Vision loss
  • Nausea, constipation or diarrhea
  • Swelling of lips, tongue or face
  • Low or high blood pressure

Normal adult breathing rate is 12 to 16 breaths per minute. You can count how many times the patient's chest rises over 1 minute. 23

Prevention

Embedded content: https://youtu.be/9jmULQ_m04o

1. Bring the patient's prescription and over-the-counter medications, vitamins, supplements and allergy list to show the doctor and nurse.1

Personal Medicine List from ISMP

2. Use a phone drug app or a website for drug information and interactions.1

Drug Information Portal from US National Library of Medicine

Drugs, Herbs, and Supplements from MedlinePlus

Drug Reaction Information from MedlinePlus

Embedded content: https://youtu.be/BJI1ToB-Dv8

3. Make sure your nurse scans your wristband to verify your name before giving you any medication if the hospital has this system.1

Embedded content: https://youtu.be/rbVO5Qc_wJ8

4. Share your hospital medication list with your family and/or advocate when changes are made.1

5. Ask for a hospital pharmacist to visit if your questions aren’t explained to you or the patient.1

6. Don’t take supplements or medications without telling your nurse and doctor.1

7. Constipation is common side effect from opioid (narcotic) medications-the patient should get bowel medications to prevent this.1

8. Report all medication reactions to your nurse at the hospital. Tell your nurse to report any medication error. Ask for the nursing supervisor or attending doctor regarding this adverse medication report.1

You or your advocate can report a medication error to: Institute for Safe Medication Practices Medication Errors Reporting Program (MERP): 1-215-947-77977 or: Institute for Safe Medication Practices

Questions to Ask

1. Do the pills look different from your medications at home?1

2. Why is your nurse giving you a medication?1

3. Can I have a copy of my or the patient's Medication Administration Record (MAR)?1

4. Have there been any changes to my medications?

5. How long do I have to take the new medications?

6. How will I be monitored on these medications?

7. What are the side effects of these medications?
